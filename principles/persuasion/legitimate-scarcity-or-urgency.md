# Legitimate urgency, not manufactured

> **Category:** Persuasion · **contextual impact**
> Work out whether the scarcity or urgency on your page is real and verifiable — or just a permanent, fictional pressure tactic.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/persuasao/escassez-ou-urgencia-legitima.md)

Scarcity and urgency — limited stock, restricted spots, deadlines — are powerful psychological triggers. But landing pages that lean on fake, permanent urgency damage trust. This guide tells legitimate urgency apart from the empty tactic.

## Why it matters

- Cialdini's scarcity principle shows that the perception of a limit narrows the decision space and speeds up action. A customer sees "last 3 spots" and moves out of fear of missing out. But it only works because it's believable: if you say there are 3 spots and there really are, the tactic is legitimate persuasion.
- When the page says "Limited offer — 48 hours" every single week, or "Last units" permanently, the visitor tunes the signal out. Worse: they notice the dishonesty and start questioning the whole pitch. Fake urgency doesn't convert; it erodes credibility.
- Legitimate scarcity is verifiable: a real number of spots, a final date that's communicated and honored, stock that moves as sales happen. Fake scarcity is fixed, permanent, with no evidence and no consequence — if the visitor comes back next week and sees it unchanged, they've learned there's no limit at all.

## How to check your page

- [ ] If you say "last X units/spots", does that number actually move with real sales or signups?
- [ ] If you name a deadline, does the page change or come down when the deadline passes?
- [ ] Is there any way to verify the limit (a visible counter, a waitlist with confirmation) — or is it just text?
- [ ] Does the urgency show up on the page week after week, or is it stated once and then removed?
- [ ] Is the scarcity trigger the only argument for converting, or is there a clear value proposition too?
- [ ] If the visitor comes back 2-4 weeks later, is the urgency message still identical and active?
- [ ] Is there supporting evidence (recent reviews, dates spots filled, "sold out" notices) that backs up the scarcity?

## Common mistakes

- **A permanent "limited offer" with no date** — The page says "Special offer for a limited time" but the wording has been identical for months. The visitor clicks back three weeks later and nothing has changed. Credibility erodes on the spot.
- **A degraded or fake stock counter** — It shows "Only 2 left" with urgency styling, but the visitor knows it's a template or saw the same number yesterday. With no real change, there's no conversion.
- **A dated deadline with no action after it** — The page announces "Enrollment closes June 15" — the visitor returns on the 17th and the page is exactly the same, same active button, same urgency message.
- **Scarcity as the only argument** — The whole page rests on "only 5 spots" without ever explaining why the spot is valuable or how candidates are chosen. Urgency stands in for a value proposition.
