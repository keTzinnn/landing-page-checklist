# Give value before you ask for anything

> **Category:** Persuasion · **contextual impact**
> Check whether the landing page offers something of concrete value (a diagnosis, material, sample) before asking for an action or data. Reciprocity drives conversion.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/persuasao/reciprocidade-oferta-de-valor.md)

When a landing page gives concrete value before asking for an action (signup, purchase, share), it activates the psychological principle of reciprocity. The visitor feels a small debt and is more inclined to give back. The challenge isn't promising a future benefit — it's delivering something tangible, verifiable, and accessible right now.

## Why it matters

- The reciprocity principle, documented in studies of human behavior, shows that people naturally feel obliged to return a favor when they receive something of value. A landing page that offers a free diagnosis, checklist, template, or working sample creates that dynamic before the sale even comes up. The visitor doesn't feel "sold to" — they feel they already got something useful.
- Pages that give value up front lower the mental barrier to the next action. A concrete example: a site that offers a free analysis of your site (even a simplified one) lets the user experience the value of the real service. It's not a promise; it's a demonstration. That difference is critical because it turns distrust into trust built on lived experience.
- Without that up-front value, the page has to lean entirely on text-based argument — "trust us". Landing-page visitors are skeptical by default. Someone arriving from an ad, an email, or a search doesn't know the brand; a free resource or tool works as behavioral proof, not just a stated claim.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the page offer something concrete for free BEFORE asking for an email or signup (checklist, diagnosis, tool, sample, report)?
- [ ] Is that offer visible and easy to access, or hidden behind a form or a deep click?
- [ ] Is the material or tool genuinely useful on its own (not an empty branded lure), or does it just list the features of the paid product?
- [ ] If there's an email/signup CTA, does it come AFTER the value offer or try to get ahead of it?
- [ ] Does the page make it clear what the visitor gets by engaging — not vague ("explore our solution") but specific ("you'll get a diagnosis in 5 minutes")?
- [ ] If a form unlocks the material, is it minimal (1-3 fields) or loaded (10+ fields, "segmentation")?
- [ ] Is there any visual proof or testimonial from someone who has used the free material or tool?

## Common mistakes

- **The value offer is hidden or secondary** — The page mentions "Request a free audit" but the button is tiny, stuck in the footer, or demands a long form (name, company, role, budget) before any contact. The result: the visitor feels sales pressure, not reciprocity.
- **Benefit promised, not delivered** — The page says "Get a free analysis", but clicking through leads to a contact form and "our team will reach out within 24h". That isn't up-front value; it's a scheduling step. The visitor gets nothing immediately and no psychological debt forms.
- **Generic or recycled free material** — It offers an e-book that's really just content already on the blog, or anything anyone could find on the web. Up-front value has to be exclusive or different from what the visitor can already get elsewhere; otherwise it sparks no reciprocity.
- **A value offer that's too vague** — "Download our guide: 50 design tips." With no cover image, clear description, or preview, the visitor can't judge whether it's worth an email. It's missing the visual cue that signals real value.
