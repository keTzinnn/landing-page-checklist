# Name the visitor's pain before the solution

> **Category:** Persuasion · **contextual impact**
> Copy that sells starts by naming the frustration before it offers the cure. The PAS structure: what the pain is, why it hurts, how to solve it. Without it, the solution has no context.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/persuasao/dor-antes-da-solucao.md)

Persuasive copy meets visitors where they are, not where you want them to be. If the page jumps straight to the solution — "We do this" — the visitor still has no reason to care. The pain comes first. The visitor recognizes their own frustration on the page, and only then accepts that your product solves it.

## Why it matters

- The copywriting structure known as Problem-Agitate-Solve maps exactly this: before you sell, you have to activate the urgency of a recognizable problem. A founder who lands on your page is thinking "my conversion rate won't budge" or "my site looks amateur" — not "I need a UX audit". If you open by talking about your service instead of their problem, you lose instant relevance.
- Relevance is the rarest currency on a landing page: the first few seconds demand continuity between the visitor's intent and what the page says. By naming the specific pain before the solution, you create message match — when visitors see themselves in your copy, they keep reading. Without it, you're writing into the void.
- The opposite risk — dwelling on the pain without delivering a solution — fails too. The page needs both moves: "you're dealing with this" (identification) and "here's the way out" (resolution). Stay stuck on the pain and you create frustration instead of hope. The order is what shifts the dynamic.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the copy above the fold name a problem the visitor recognizes as their own — or is it too generic ("make everything better")?
- [ ] Before describing what the company does, is there a passage that articulates why the problem stings right now — the friction or the cost of not fixing it?
- [ ] Is the move from pain to solution clear — not jumping from "your site is bad" straight to "buy our product", but connecting: "that's why we built X"?
- [ ] If the page targets a specific audience (SaaS founder, marketer, designer), is the pain specific to that audience — or could it apply to anyone?
- [ ] Is there any sign the pain is real (sales calls, visitor feedback, a pattern seen on similar pages) — or is it just an assumption?

## Common mistakes

- **Starting with the solution, not the pain** — "A UX audit of your landing page in 2 minutes." The visitor didn't arrive thinking about audits — they arrived because their rate won't climb. Jump straight to the mechanism and you lose the relevance hook.
- **Pain that's too generic** — "Everyone wants a better website." Far too vague. People don't see themselves in it. Specific: "Your form has 7 fields — visitors leave without filling it" sparks instant recognition.
- **Pain with no clear way out** — The page laments ("the market is competitive, your rate is low") but never makes it obvious how your product fixes it. The visitor is left with frustration, not hope.
- **Borrowing a competitor's pain without checking it** — You assume "SaaS founders want exponential growth" because you saw it on another landing page, but your real audience never said that. Pain has to be grounded in actual conversations, not market guesswork.
