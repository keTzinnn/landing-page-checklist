# Demonstrated authority

> **Category:** Persuasion · **contextual impact**
> Visible signals of authority — certifications, press, recognizable clients, hard numbers — reduce uncertainty and build a visitor's trust.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/persuasao/autoridade-demonstrada.md)

People decide in seconds. When they see tangible proof that you know your craft — press that covered you, real clients listed, recognized certifications, numbers that document results — the brain stops doubting and moves on. Demonstrated authority isn't about looking important. It's about removing the mental friction that stalls a conversion.

## Why it matters

- Cialdini called it social proof: human brains lean on mental shortcuts to decide who to trust. When someone respected — or a verifiable number — vouches for you, visitors believe it faster than when you say it yourself.
- Landing pages that skip authority signals force visitors to fill the gaps by guessing at your qualifications. That burns cognitive energy and adds friction. Visible proof — client logos, mentions in recognized outlets, active-user counts — closes those gaps before the objection is even born.
- A complete absence of authority signals triggers defensive reading: the visitor switches on the skepticism filter. Even simple credentials ("member of Association X" or "featured in YZ") switch that filter off.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does your page show recognizable clients or brands? Logos, names, or case studies of names the visitor knows?
- [ ] Is there any mention of press, awards, or recognition from established outlets? (Real, not fabricated.)
- [ ] Do industry-relevant certifications or memberships appear visually? (e.g. ISO, a professional body, an official seal.)
- [ ] Do verifiable numbers or facts back up your authority? (e.g. "since 2015", "10,000 users", "X years of experience".)
- [ ] Are known experts or respected figures in your field credited in relation to your work? (Name, title, and context visible — not anonymous.)

## Common mistakes

- **Generic or invisible credentials** — Text buried in the footer that says "certified" with no logo or link. Move it to a trust section above the fold or next to your main CTA.
- **Real clients listed without names or logos** — You have notable clients but you write "one of the 500 largest companies" without saying which. Show recognizable names or logos; anonymity kills credibility.
- **Numbers with no context or way to verify** — Dropping "3 million users" with no time frame, source, or link to check sounds fabricated. Be specific: "since 2018" or "in 12 months".
- **Press mentioned with no link or date** — "Featured in major media" is vague. Show the outlet, the date, and a link; it converts better than something that reads as made up.
