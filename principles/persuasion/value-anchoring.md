# Anchor the value before the price conversation

> **Category:** Persuasion · **contextual impact**
> Perceived value depends on the reference point shown first. Prices, comparisons, and before/after framing anchor the decision.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/persuasao/ancoragem-de-valor.md)

When visitors land on your page, they don't judge your price in a vacuum. They look for a reference point — a previous price, a competitor's plan, a "recommended" figure — and use that number to frame everything that follows. If you don't supply the anchor, visitors invent their own, and it usually works against you.

## Why it matters

- Anchoring is a well-documented cognitive bias: the first number people see disproportionately shapes how they read every number after it. When a visitor sees a R$ 500/month plan before considering yours at R$ 200/month, the second option feels like more value — not because it changed, but because the anchor did. Cialdini showed that even arbitrary anchors sway buying decisions, and Ogilvy's take on price copy makes the same point: don't sell the number alone, sell where it sits in the market.
- No reference point breeds ambiguity. A page that shows only the final price — no comparison, no value breakdown, no clarity on the alternatives — forces visitors to do more work, and decisions made under uncertainty convert worse. Cutting that cognitive load lifts your action rate, and a good anchor does exactly that: it answers the unspoken question "is this a good price?" before the visitor even has to ask it.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Is there a clear price anchor in the first view (a previous price, a competitor, or a "recommended" figure)?
- [ ] Is your price framed relatively — e.g. "save 40%" or "X% cheaper than Y"?
- [ ] Is there a visual breakdown (table, cards, or section) showing several options with prices side by side?
- [ ] If you offer a discount, is the original price shown struck through, or do you only show the final number?
- [ ] Does your price copy use the language of savings or status ("most popular choice" or "investment")?
- [ ] Is there a clear time anchor ("per month", "billed annually, save X", "one-time payment")?

## Common mistakes

- **A floating price with no context** — Showing just "R$ 299/month" with no reference to alternatives, no percentage off, no original price. Visitors can't tell whether it's expensive or cheap and leave unsure.
- **An anchor that's invisible or too far away** — The anchor (a competitor's price, a comparison table) sits in a section nowhere near the buy CTA. Visitors never connect it to the price at the top.
- **Comparing against an irrelevant option** — The anchor is a far pricier product (out of the visitor's world) or a far cheaper one (a different category). The reference loses credibility and the effect flips.
- **Discount shown, original price missing** — Showing "Now R$ 99" without ever showing "was R$ 199". Visitors see no savings — just a number with no context.
