# Lead with a number, not a vague promise

> **Category:** Persuasion · **contextual impact**
> At least one benefit is concrete and quantified (a number, a time frame, a result) — not a vague promise. Superlative claims with nothing behind them count against you.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/persuasao/beneficio-tangivel-quantificado.md)

Vague promises ("the best solution", "revolutionary", "game-changing") don't move conversions. Visitors need to know: what's the result in numbers, time, or a clear metric? When copy translates a benefit into concrete quantities — time saved, cost cut, speed gained — that tangible value cuts friction and uncertainty.

## Why it matters

- Specific claims (a number, a time frame, a unit) are more believable and more memorable than generic promises. Visitors distrust superlatives with nothing behind them — it's the classic tell of empty puffery. A quantified benefit reads as more legitimate and defuses the unspoken objection ("is that really true?"): the number gives the visitor something concrete to check.
- When the page says "save 5 hours a week" instead of "save time", visitors can picture the result in their own lives. When it promises "30-day trial" instead of "try it out", it lowers the fear of risk. Concreteness cuts cognitive friction.
- Without quantification, visitors fill the gap themselves — and the mind fills it conservatively. Superlatives with no number ("the best") trigger skepticism, not trust. The trade-off is clear: specificity costs you some up-front credibility but wins the conversion, because it removes uncertainty.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the page carry at least one benefit as a number, time frame, or clear metric (e.g. "cuts it by 40%", "in 2 minutes", "saves 8 hours")?
- [ ] Do superlatives ("best", "only", "revolutionary", "amazing") appear WITHOUT a number or tangible proof to anchor them?
- [ ] Is your main conversion promise (headline or CTA) vague ("transform your life", "complete solution") or pinned to a measurable result?
- [ ] If you compare to competitors or use before/after, are the numbers plausible and relatable (or just generic, like "faster")?
- [ ] Is it clear WHICH result the customer gets — not what the tool does, but their gain in a tangible unit?

## Common mistakes

- **"The best solution on the market"** — A superlative with no anchor. Visitors think "says who?" or "best by what metric?". With no number or proof, it triggers skepticism. Fix: "Cuts setup time from 3 hours to 15 minutes" or "Recommended by X customers".
- **"Revolutionary", "transformative", "game-changer"** — Pure puffery. These supposedly universal words are empty without specifics. Visitors read them as hype. Fix: spell out the concrete gain — "audit your landing pages in 2 minutes instead of 2 days".
- **Generic comparison: "faster", "better quality", "cheaper"** — Relative with no number. Faster by how much? With no baseline or metric, it's empty advertising. Fix: "5x faster than doing it by hand" or "loads in under 2 seconds".
- **A number with no context: "We process 10 million events"** — Internal volume isn't a customer benefit. Visitors don't care about the tool's scale — they care about their result. Fix: "Processes your data in under 5 minutes" (their wait) or "cuts infrastructure cost by 40%".
