# Small first ask, commitment that builds

> **Category:** Persuasion · **contextual impact**
> The visitor starts with a micro-commitment (newsletter, free demo) before you ask for the high-effort conversion (purchase, contract).
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/persuasao/compromisso-progressivo.md)

A low-commitment first ask creates psychological momentum: someone who agrees to leave an email or watch a video is more likely to say yes to the next, bigger ask. It's the opposite of the common mistake — asking for the purchase (or the qualified lead, or the paid trial) before you've built the progression.

## Why it matters

- Someone who says yes to something small feels a pull toward consistency: they want to stay in line with the action they just took and the self-image they formed by taking it. This isn't manipulation — it's recognizing that every human decision carries psychological weight. One small action leaves a mark.
- The mistake is asking for the heavy conversion on arrival: "Buy now", "Sign a contract", "Talk to sales today". With no progression, the visitor faces a high exit barrier from the first click — many leave with no commitment at all, zero momentum, no reason to come back.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the page offer an optional, low-friction first step before any heavy conversion (newsletter signup, access to a guide or template, a no-card trial)?
- [ ] Is the micro-commitment clear and immediately rewarding — not vague ("learn more") or conditional ("send us your details and maybe we'll be in touch")?
- [ ] After the visitor accepts the micro-commitment, is the next action (the high-effort conversion) framed as a natural next step rather than a bait-and-switch?
- [ ] Does the page avoid making the heavy conversion (purchase, sales call, contract) the ONLY way in?
- [ ] Is the free first step a good fit for the visitor's profile — or does it cost you credibility by seeming "too easy"?

## Common mistakes

- **Asking for the heavy conversion on arrival** — The page shows a hero with a single CTA: "Buy now", "Sign up today", "Book a demo with sales". The visitor has never done anything with the brand — they'll hit high resistance. No progressive commitment.
- **A micro-commitment that goes nowhere** — The visitor leaves an email for a newsletter, then gets generic spam — or nothing. The small "yes" never turns into a bigger commitment because the promise went unkept, and that kills credibility.
- **Disguising a data grab as a benefit** — "Unlock our report — fill in 7 fields." That's a commitment, not a micro-commitment. The visitor knows they're being qualified (and may never get the report anyway — "we'll review your request").
