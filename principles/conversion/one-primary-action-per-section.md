# One primary action per section

> **Category:** Conversion · **high impact**
> Multiple CTAs competing confuse the visitor. When the next step is ambiguous, conversion collapses.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/conversao/cta-unico-por-secao.md)

Visitors on a landing page are like drivers on a highway: they want to know exactly which way to go. When a section offers two or more equally prominent paths — 'Start free trial,' 'See pricing,' 'Talk to sales' — the visitor hesitates, processes longer, and often leaves. One CTA per section removes the decision noise and keeps conversion momentum.

## Why it matters

- The principle rests on Hick's Law: the more options a user faces at once, the longer they take to decide. On a landing page, that time turns into doubt. A section devoted to 'Why uxaudit?' shouldn't offer both 'Learn more' and 'Buy now' in the same space — the first dilutes the educational purpose, the second confuses with a premature sell. One focal CTA per section keeps the intent clear and the journey moving forward.
- Landing pages live on momentum. The visitor arrives with a level of interest. Each section should deepen or consolidate that interest toward a concrete next step. The 'Features' section can invite 'Explore in detail'; the 'Social proof' section can invite 'Start a free audit'; the 'Pricing' section can invite 'Choose a plan.' That progression only works if no section competes with itself — otherwise the visitor restarts the calculation at every touchpoint.
- Pages with multiple equally prominent CTAs in one section see higher drop-off between the first and second click. The visitor clicks 'Learn more,' goes to another page, comes back, sees 'Book a demo,' and questions whether 'Learn more' was the right path. Pages with one CTA per section hold visual and typographic coherence and convert better because they cut decision friction.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does each section (benefits, social proof, pricing, FAQ) have exactly ONE button or link styled as the primary action?
- [ ] If a section has multiple actions, is only one primary (visually dominant) and the rest secondary (subtle, inline, or lower visual weight)?
- [ ] Does the CTA shift or evolve by section (e.g. 'Explore' → 'Try' → 'Buy'), or repeat the same action everywhere?
- [ ] Are there secondary links (footer, navbar) that compete visually with the active section's primary CTA?
- [ ] Can the visitor tell, in under 2 seconds, what the expected action is in that section without reading microcopy?

## Common mistakes

- **Multiple colored buttons side by side** — A benefits section with three equally prominent buttons ('Start,' 'See pricing,' 'Talk to sales'), all with the same visual weight. The visitor wants to click one, but the others fight for attention — so they hesitate.
- **CTA in the text + CTA in the button + CTA in the banner** — The same 'Why choose us' paragraph carries multiple action triggers: an inline link, a prominent button, and a floating banner. Three paths, one section, choice paralysis.
- **Secondary CTA as prominent as the primary** — 'Start free trial' (visually dominant) sits beside 'Browse the docs' (also visible and clickable at the same level). The equal visual weight blurs the priority.
