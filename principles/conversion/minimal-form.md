# A form that asks only for the essentials

> **Category:** Conversion · **high impact**
> Minimal fields, with microcopy justifying every sensitive question, to cut friction and drop-offs before conversion.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/conversao/formulario-minimo.md)

The more fields a form asks for, the lower the completion rate. Every extra field is a psychological barrier. The minimal-form principle demands it: ask only for data you truly need, use microcopy to justify sensitive fields (phone, tax ID, company), and cut false optionality. Visitors feel less friction, abandon less, and conversion rises.

## Why it matters

- Long forms create cognitive friction. Hick's Law says more options (or fields) increase decision time and abandonment. Fewer fields means lower psychological cost, especially for sensitive ones like phone, tax ID, or company. Users hesitate when they fear spam or a data leak. Without microcopy explaining why you need it, distrust beats the urge to convert.
- Microcopy works because it humanizes the friction. 'Phone: so we can reach you after approval (we don't sell your number)' lowers the fear of spam. 'Tax ID: legally required for B2B contracts' justifies the intrusion. Without that clarity, the user imagines worst-case scenarios. Imagined fear is stronger than evidence-based fear — microcopy interrupts the bad story and rebuilds trust.
- Minimal forms also raise data quality. Fewer fields means fewer wrong autofills, fewer emails typed in a rush. And they make testing easier: you can test variations fast (5 vs. 8 fields, with or without microcopy) and measure real impact on conversion.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Are you asking for a full name when first name alone would do? Or for company when it could come after conversion?
- [ ] Does every sensitive field (phone, tax ID, banking data) have microcopy explaining why you need it and how the data will be used?
- [ ] Have you visually distinguished required fields from optional ones? Optional fields should look optional, not like traps.
- [ ] Has the form been tested to measure drop-off by field — which field do most people quit on?
- [ ] Could any field move to after conversion (e.g. billing details after the purchase is confirmed)?
- [ ] Does the field order follow a logic: simple personal data first, sensitive or complex data later?
- [ ] Have you checked whether the current microcopy actually reduces friction, or just reads like corporate jargon nobody reads?

## Common mistakes

- **A form with 15+ fields on one screen** — The user sees a wall of questions and quits before starting. Fix: split it into two screens, or cut to the essentials (name, email, phone). Extras like company can come after conversion. Expected result: more completed submissions.
- **Phone or tax ID with no explanatory microcopy** — The user thinks 'why do they want this?', fears their data will be sold, and leaves. Add one line: 'Phone: so we can reach you after approval. Your number is never sold or shared.' Costs zero space, saves conversions.
- **Required 'Company' field on a consumer form** — An everyday consumer has no company. You force an empty answer, the field fails validation, the conversion breaks. Leave it out or switch to 'Job title (optional).'
- **Generic error after submit** — The user fills everything in, submits, and sees 'Error: complete the required fields' without saying which. They get frustrated and close the tab. Validate in real time per field (inline) and show the specific error before they hit submit.
