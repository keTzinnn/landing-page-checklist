# A top that's free of focus-stealing distractions

> **Category:** Conversion · **high impact**
> Above the fold there are no navigation/menu links or modals pulling focus away from conversion.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/conversao/sem-distracao-de-saida.md)

A well-made landing page doesn't compete with itself. When the visitor lands above the fold — the first screen they see without scrolling — they need a clear narrative and a single path to action, not multiple escape routes (nav menus, secondary links, tabs, or modals). Landing pages that behave like websites lose conversion simply because they split attention.

## Why it matters

- The visitor has limited attention cycles. Eye-tracking research on landing pages shows the above-the-fold region acts as a visual contract: the user forms an expectation in the first few seconds. If they see a full nav menu, footer links, or a modal, their brain reads the page as a website — generic, with many destinations — and their motivation to complete the primary action drops. Dedicated landings create urgency through simplicity.
- Secondary navigation (horizontal menus, tabs, section links) forces the visitor into a cognitive choice: stay in the intended narrative or branch off elsewhere. That's convertible friction. Every alternative option is an exit. The AIDA model (Attention → Interest → Desire → Action) depends on progressively narrowing options, not multiplying them.
- Modals (newsletter pop-ups, aggressive cookie CTAs, promos) above the fold are worse still: they don't just split attention, they physically interrupt the narrative. Abandonment research shows that the presence of an overlay reduces completion of conversion actions — even when the visitor can close it — because it creates immediate friction. Any interstitial is only viable if it's legitimate (like a required cookie consent) and discreetly placed, not blocking.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Is there a horizontal or vertical nav menu visible above the fold offering links to sections, blog, about, or contact that aren't the primary action?
- [ ] Is there a modal, pop-up, or overlay covering content or the CTA on initial load (an aggressive cookie CTA, newsletter, upsell)?
- [ ] Does the page show multiple tabs, sliders with clickable subtitles, or cards with competing CTAs above the fold, with no clear hierarchy?
- [ ] Can the visitor identify the expected action on first visual contact (fill the form, click the trial button, etc.), or are there multiple equally prominent routes?
- [ ] Are secondary nav links or breadcrumbs present in the hero/above the fold, offering alternative paths?

## Common mistakes

- **Nav menu at the top of the hero** — A SaaS landing opens with a horizontal menu listing Product, Pricing, Blog, Contact. The visitor has multiple exit options before even reading the main proposition, lowering engagement with the conversion narrative.
- **Newsletter pop-up fired on load** — A 'Join the VIP list!' modal covers the visible hero region on arrival. Even though it's closable, the visitor has already registered an obstacle in their journey and grows less willing to continue.
- **Competing tabs or sliders above the fold** — An e-commerce site shows 'Sale | New | Best Sellers' as clickable tabs in the hero. The visitor veers off into a secondary path, drifting away from the central proposed action.
- **Secondary links in the hero header** — An app landing shows 'App Download | Web App | Blog | Roadmap' up top. Those routes compete visually with the primary action, and the visitor gets pulled into side exploration instead of engaging with the conversion.
