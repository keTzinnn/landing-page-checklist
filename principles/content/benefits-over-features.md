# Customer gains over features

> **Category:** Content · **medium-low impact**
> Why "cloud backup" doesn't sell but "sleep easy knowing your data is safe" does. How to avoid the feature-list trap on your landing page.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/conteudo/beneficios-sobre-features.md)

The gap between selling a feature and selling a customer gain is subtle, but loud. "Real-time analysis" is what you built; "catch problems before your customers do" is the gain the visitor buys. A landing page that lists features without translating them into gains reads like a spec sheet. One that shows the gain (the result, the relief, the power) makes the product both understandable and desirable.

## Why it matters

- Features are the technical answer; benefits are the emotional and economic one. Visitors don't browse your page looking for specs, they browse it to solve a problem or reach a result. When you talk in features ("REST API", "customizable dashboard", "2FA"), the visitor has to translate each one into their own context, and every mental translation you force on them raises the odds they bounce. When you talk in benefits ("go live in minutes", "see what matters at a glance", "sleep easy"), they recognize the payoff instantly, no work required.
- Features have a shelf life. The feature you announce today is copied tomorrow by any competitor. Benefits, when tied to your real differentiator, are harder to copy. "The tool reports in real time" is a feature; "thanks to real-time reporting, you stop wasting time monitoring by hand" is a benefit only your seasoned customers fully get, which is exactly why it spreads among them.
- The classic mistake is assuming the visitor knows your industry the way you do. B2B SaaS is the worst offender: the pressure to sound "professional" and "complete" produces pages that read like spec manuals. The less a visitor knows your space, the more translation they need, and a benefit does that translation on the spot.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does every feature you list sit next to (or just before) its "so what for the user" answer? For example: "Slack integration → ping the team in real time without leaving the tool."
- [ ] Do your headings and subheads talk in gains or in features? ("Work faster" vs. "Parallel processing".)
- [ ] Do your "how it works" sections show both the steps (feature) and the result (gain), or do they stop at the technical walkthrough?
- [ ] Is there a section that translates the technical jargon into plain language, or do you assume the visitor knows what "webhook", "single sign-on" or "rate limiting" mean?
- [ ] In your testimonials, do customers talk about the result they live, or just list the features they like?
- [ ] Is your headline promise a gain ("never notify by hand again") or a feature ("automatic notifications")?

## Common mistakes

- **Feature list with no payoff** — "Integrates with 200+ apps · Customizable dashboard · PDF reports · 2FA." With no context, it's a catalog. The visitor reads it, forgets it, leaves.
- **Jargon dressed up as a benefit** — "Machine learning for churn prediction." If the visitor isn't a data scientist, that's not a benefit, it's an acronym. The benefit is "know who's about to leave before they walk out the door."
- **Feature buried inside a benefit** — "Gain speed" (gain) immediately followed by "through our async event-stream processing architecture" (needless feature). The visitor reads it, gets confused, and docks your credibility.
- **Comparison that turns into a spec sheet** — An "Us vs. Them" table of feature checkmarks the visitor has no way to weigh. The real difference ("support in your language", "hosted locally") stays invisible.
