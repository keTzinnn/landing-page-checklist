# The customer's voice in your copy

> **Category:** Content · **medium-low impact**
> Your copy speaks the customer's language, their problems, desires and results in the audience's own words, not internal jargon. It measures whether the copy echoes the customer.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/conteudo/voz-do-cliente.md)

A landing page that truly converts doesn't talk to itself, it talks to the person reading. The gap between copy that echoes the customer and copy that just explains the product is the gap between a page that sells and one that doesn't. This principle measures whether your headline, your benefits and your objection answers use the same problems, pains and vocabulary the customer already uses when searching for the solution.

## Why it matters

- Customers don't think in features, they think in results that matter to them right now. When you say "workflow automation" but they're saying "I need to stop doing this by hand every single day," there's a gap. That gap makes your copy invisible. Internal jargon sounds vague, even suspect. Copy that echoes the customer, using "without doing it by hand," "save hours," "stop thinking about repetitive tasks," touches their real fear and desire. Cialdini's similarity principle shows the customer feels mirrored in copy when they recognize their own language.
- Customer research proves the best pages inherit their language straight from testimonials, support emails and community comments, not internal brainstorms. When you say what the customer is already saying, resistance drops. Objections get answered in language they recognize. Social proof gains credibility because the tone matches their reality. Generic copy ("innovative solution," "digital transformation") leaves the visitor unsure. Copy that echoes the customer removes that doubt because it names the exact problems they have.
- This is different from "no jargon," which measures noise. A page can be jargon-free and still be generic: "we do it better, faster." A page that echoes the customer says: "you'll stop wasting time on X and get to do the Y that actually matters." It's the difference between technical and empathetic, anchored in reality.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the headline use problems or results the customer already articulates (not company terms)?
- [ ] Do the benefits at the top echo specific pains mentioned in testimonials or customer research?
- [ ] When answering objections, does it use the language and concerns that come up naturally in customer conversations?
- [ ] Does the copy avoid internal terms (feature names, in-house acronyms, corporate-speak) and speak in gains from the customer's world?
- [ ] Do the examples or use cases use the context and language of the target segment, not generic scenarios?

## Common mistakes

- **Copy written in the company's private dialect** — An event-management SaaS uses "workflow orchestration" in the hero. In research, the customer says "I lose hours doing this by hand every day." The word "orchestration" appears in zero testimonials. The copy should say: "save hours on repetitive tasks", the language the customer uses to describe the problem.
- **Benefits with no root in real pain** — An online-training page promises "transform your career with future-ready skills." Research shows real customers bring sharper urgencies: "I want to earn more this year," "I'm falling behind my peers," "I need a credential the market respects." Generic copy leaves the visitor thinking "maybe." Copy that echoes: "a market-recognized credential you can earn in 3 months" (answers pains customers actually voiced).
- **Social proof that doesn't echo the customer** — A testimonial reads: "Innovative solution, highly recommend" (generic). The target customer wants to know: "does this fit my situation?", "how long does it take?", "what was the gain?". An effective testimonial: "I cut hours of repetitive support. My team is 5 people. I'd tried 3 tools before.", it reflects the reader's pains and context.
- **Objection answered in corporate language** — The page anticipates: "Worried about integration?" and answers: "API-agnostic architecture." The customer was thinking: "will it be a hassle to connect to the system we already use?" Right answer: "connects in 5 minutes, no code, with [the tool you already use]." Same concern, different language, the customer doesn't talk in "agnostic webhooks."
