# Objections answered on the page

> **Category:** Content · **medium-low impact**
> Copy that anticipates objections doesn't hide them, it answers them before the question even comes up.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/conteudo/tratamento-de-objecoes.md)

Most landing pages let the reader think "this isn't for me" or "it's too expensive" without ever saying why out loud. Copy that anticipates objections doesn't bury them, it answers them before the question forms. This isn't a trick; it's information design that cuts cognitive friction and keeps attention on the goal.

## Why it matters

- Readers carry silent doubts and leave when the value isn't obvious in the natural flow of reading. Unspoken objections ("will this be expensive?", "does it work for my case?", "how much risk is there?") interrupt reading and pull attention away. Copy that anticipates them cuts that noise and improves the flow of understanding.
- Cialdini's reciprocity principle shows that answering an objection the reader hasn't even raised builds trust. It's as if you said "I understand your concern" without making them voice it. That signals honesty and lowers their sales radar.
- In SaaS and digital services, the three most common objections are: (1) price/cost, (2) fit for the use case, (3) risk/guarantee. A landing page that ignores any one of them leaves the door open for the visitor to leave and shop around. Answering each one, concisely and anchored in a real differentiator, closes those exit gaps.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the page name the biggest objection out loud (e.g. "Is it expensive?" / "Will it work for my business?" / "What's the risk?") and answer it with visible evidence?
- [ ] Are the objection answers free of empty marketing ("it's not expensive for the value it delivers") and instead anchored in observable criteria (price comparison, specific use cases, a guarantee or refund policy)?
- [ ] Does the page distinguish fit (who's the ideal case vs. who isn't) instead of trying to convince everyone? (e.g. "This is ideal if you test landing pages. If your site is a catalog with no conversion goal, it may not be for you.")
- [ ] Are secondary objections (setup time, training, support) addressed before the CTA, right where the wavering visitor looks for reassurance?
- [ ] Is the tone of the answers honest and matter-of-fact, not defensive or hyperbolic?

## Common mistakes

- **Ignoring the obvious objection** — The page says "Easy UX auditing" but never answers "easy how?" or "easy for whom? Do I need to code?". The reader is left wondering and leaves. Answer it: "No code needed. You drop in the URL, we analyze it. 5 minutes."
- **Answering too generically** — Objection: "What if I don't like it?" Weak answer: "Satisfaction guaranteed!" Right answer: "Try it free for 7 days, no card, on real cases from your own business. Then decide." Concrete evidence that lowers the risk.
- **Denying instead of acknowledging** — The visitor thinks "too expensive" and the copy says "it's cheap!", a head-on contradiction that sounds defensive. Better: "Plans start at $X/month. Run fewer than 5 tests a month and another plan may suit you; run 20+ and the ROI lands in 2 weeks." It acknowledges the objection and qualifies.
- **Leaving the objection to the FAQ or footer** — Someone wavering in the middle of the page won't jump to the FAQ. Answer it right there, in the natural flow of reading, before attention slips away.
