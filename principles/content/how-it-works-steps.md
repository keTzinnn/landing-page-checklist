# A clear path in steps

> **Category:** Content · **medium-low impact**
> The page shows the journey in clear steps (usually 3) from the customer's point of view, from click to result (the "how it works").
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/conteudo/plano-de-passos.md)

Visitors who can't picture how to use your product leave. A step plan turns the abstract into something concrete: paste the URL → get the result → act on the insight. When the path is visible and numbered, the visitor feels in control and confident. It's the difference between "we're software" (generic) and "you do X, then Y, then Z, and you're done" (tangible).

## Why it matters

- Visitors land with an unspoken question: "how does this actually work for me?" Without a clear map, they fill in the answer themselves, and they usually land on "this looks complicated." A step plan answers that question before it hardens into an objection. The classic shape (start, middle, result) mirrors how people describe processes: "I do this, then that happens, then I get the result." User-centered design research shows people run mental scenarios before deciding; a step plan shows them the real one.
- Numbered steps lower anxiety. When you see "3 steps," your brain knows how much effort to expect. Without that frame, every line of copy can feel like more work, that's Hick's Law in action. Steps also create a narrative: setup (what you'll do), climax (the result happening) and payoff (what you walk away with). That arc reads clearly across any culture or language.
- For products and services, especially SaaS and software, showing the path lowers the friction of trying it. When the visitor has already pictured the flow, they don't discover it mid-trial, they expected it. It's the difference between trying a product knowing what's coming and getting blindsided by surprises. The second visitor bounces; the first converts.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Are the steps numbered or clearly separated (1, 2, 3) above the fold or in a dedicated section?
- [ ] Is each step described in 1-2 plain lines, with no jargon or side details?
- [ ] Is the end result clear, what the visitor will have, know, or be able to do after step 3?
- [ ] Are the steps in the order the customer actually goes through, not the order that makes sense internally?
- [ ] Is there an image, icon or diagram for each step, or is the text alone scannable enough on its own?

## Common mistakes

- **Steps that describe the product, not the customer's flow** — Example: "Step 1: Connect your data tool. Step 2: Our engine processes it. Step 3: Get reports." The customer can't see themselves, they're reading about the machine. Fix: "Step 1: Paste your page's URL. Step 2: Get the diagnosis in 2 minutes. Step 3: Apply the highest-impact fix." Now the customer is the one acting.
- **More than 4 steps, or steps that run long** — When the list grows, the visitor slides back to "this is complicated" and loses the visual clarity. Three steps is the gold rule; four still works; five or more and you're back to abstraction. If you have more, they're sub-steps inside one step, fix the hierarchy.
- **Steps with no tangible result at the end** — If step three ends with "use the platform" or "enjoy the benefits," the customer never pictured a real change in their world. End on a concrete outcome: "uncover the 3 biggest conversion barriers" or "find and fix the problem costing you the most money."
- **Steps cut off from the rest of the message** — The step section doesn't connect to the promise above it, it reads like a walled-off block, not continuous narrative. Tie the hero to the first step ("Here's how easy it is:") and the step 3 result to the CTA below ("Ready to start?").
