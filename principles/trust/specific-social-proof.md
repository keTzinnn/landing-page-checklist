# Testimonials with a real name attached

> **Category:** Trust · **medium impact**
> A testimonial with no name, role, or company won't convince anyone. What specific social proof is, how to audit yours, and the mistakes that destroy credibility.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/confianca/prova-social-especifica.md)

Social proof only works when it's believable. A testimonial with a name, role, company, or photo is verifiable — the visitor can confirm that person actually exists. An anonymous quote ('a happy customer') asks for trust instead of building it: in practice, it's worth close to nothing.

## Why it matters

- Social proof is one of Robert Cialdini's principles of persuasion: faced with uncertainty, people decide by looking at what others like them have already decided. It's a mental shortcut — and the shortcut only fires when the evidence looks real.
- Specificity is what separates evidence from decoration. 'We boosted conversion' says nothing; 'we went from 1.8% to 3.1% in six weeks — Name, role at Company' gives the visitor something to hold onto. The more verifiable anchors (full name, role, company, photo, link), the more credit the testimonial earns.
- The reverse effect is the underrated danger: a testimonial that looks fabricated doesn't add zero — it subtracts. Once a visitor doubts one testimonial, they start discounting every other promise on the page.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does every testimonial have a full name plus at least one verifiable identifier (role, company, profile, or a real photo)?
- [ ] Are the photos of actual people — not stock images?
- [ ] Does the text describe a concrete situation or result, or is it praise that would fit any company?
- [ ] Do client logos reflect a real relationship (a case, a project, a public mention) — or are they just there to add weight?
- [ ] Do aggregate numbers ('500+ customers') have context that makes them credible?
- [ ] No customers yet? Does the page offer another honest anchor (public demo, guarantee, your own documented result) instead of a generic testimonial?

## Common mistakes

- **Anonymous quote** — 'Excellent service!' — A happy customer. No name, no face, no way to verify: it reads as filler.
- **Stock photo** — A generic stock face with a common name underneath gives off exactly the impression you want to avoid: a staged testimonial.
- **Interchangeable praise** — A testimonial that would fit any website ('highly recommend') proves nothing about YOUR product. Specificity is what convinces.
- **Inventing testimonials** — Worse than having none: beyond being unethical, it's unsustainable — once the page loses credibility, no copy can win it back.
