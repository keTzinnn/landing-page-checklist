# An easy-to-find privacy policy

> **Category:** Trust · **medium impact**
> Can't find your privacy policy? You're breaking the law and killing trust at the same time. How to make it easy to find.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/confianca/politica-privacidade-acessivel.md)

A privacy policy that exists but is buried so deep that visitors can't find it is worse than useless: it breaks compliance and amplifies distrust. If someone has to go on a treasure hunt through 8px footer text to find it, the intent looks hidden. This principle is about accessibility — not just that the policy exists, but that it's easy to find and easy to read.

## Why it matters

- Privacy laws like Brazil's LGPD and the EU's GDPR require the privacy policy to be 'easily accessible' — an obvious, clickable link, not a hidden one. A policy set in 6px gray footer text fails the spirit of the law even if it technically exists. From a trust standpoint, it reinforces the sense that the company is hiding its terms.
- Per the Nielsen Norman Group, visitors want to see the privacy policy before sharing sensitive data. One that's easy to find, read, and understand lowers objection; one that's confusing, vague, or hard to locate amplifies suspicion. The effort to find it equals perceived concealment.
- For landing pages with a form, the privacy policy is especially critical. A visitor who sees a clear link thinks, 'OK, they're transparent.' One who finds it hidden thinks, 'they're hiding something.' The difference is small, but it compounds across conversions.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Is there a visible link to a 'Privacy Policy' (or 'Terms & Privacy') on the page?
- [ ] Is the link somewhere easy to find — footer, near the CTA, or in the contact section (not buried in 6px text)?
- [ ] Is the link clickable and working, not broken or pointing to an empty page?
- [ ] When opened, is the policy a readable document (not an image, not code, legible on mobile)?
- [ ] If you collect data, does the policy spell out what's collected and why?
- [ ] If you integrate third parties (email, analytics), does the policy disclose that?

## Common mistakes

- **Hidden in tiny gray footer text** — A 'Privacy Policy' link in 8px gray in the bottom-right corner defeats the whole point of accessibility. Set it in readable body size (12–14px), adequate contrast, and a clear spot.
- **Broken or dead link** — Worse than no link at all. The visitor clicks, hits a 404 or blank page, trust drops, and compliance is broken. Always test: does the link work, and does the page exist?
- **Wall of legalese** — A policy written as one impenetrable block of legal text technically complies, but no one reads it. Use short paragraphs, clear sections, and a summary up top. Plain language isn't illegal.
- **'Terms' referenced without a link** — A form that says 'by clicking you agree to our Terms' with no clickable link leaves the visitor agreeing to something they can't read. Always turn 'Terms' into a link to the real document.
