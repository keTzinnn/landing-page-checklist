# Price transparency

> **Category:** Trust · **medium impact**
> With no visible price, visitors assume it's expensive or that there's a catch. How to show the price (or a range) to build trust.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/confianca/transparencia-de-preco.md)

One of the simplest and most powerful objections is 'how much does it cost?'. When a landing page hides that number — gating it behind a form, a 'talk to sales' click, or an email — it creates needless friction. The visitor assumes one of two things: either the price is so high they need to filter prospects, or the company doesn't trust the price enough to show it. Either way, trust takes a hit. This article covers why price transparency works and how to audit it.

## Why it matters

- Economists call this 'information asymmetry'. When the seller withholds a price the buyer knows they should have, suspicion grows. Baymard Institute's checkout-abandonment research finds that a frequent cause is an absent or vague final price until the end of the process. Visitors who know the price upfront convert more because they've removed one variable of uncertainty.
- Psychologically, transparency signals confidence. A company that shows its price is saying, 'I trust you'll still convert.' One that hides it is saying, 'I need to drag you in before revealing it.' The second instantly lowers trust. Nielsen Norman has noted that trusted sites (Amazon, Stripe) reveal price immediately; suspect sites never do.
- For SaaS or subscription products, showing a range ('from $99/mo') or priced plans is the norm. Hiding it creates friction. Even for consulting, a 'typical investment: $10k–$50k' beats silence, because it reduces surprise and sets expectations.

## How to check your page

- [ ] If there's an offer or product, is the price (exact or a range) visible above the fold or in a dedicated 'Pricing' section?
- [ ] For SaaS/subscription, is there a plan table with the monthly/annual price clearly marked?
- [ ] If the price varies by context (volume, customization), is there an anchor ('starting at') or a price calculator?
- [ ] Is the price the final amount, or does it warn of add-ons ('plus setup', 'implementation fee')?
- [ ] If it's a lead magnet or free consultation, is that explicit — with no hidden charge as a surprise?
- [ ] For complex products, is there an FAQ or a 'How does pricing work?' section to clear up questions?

## Common mistakes

- **No price, just 'request a demo'** — With no price at all — only 'request a demo' or 'talk to sales' — the visitor bails at the click. Always reveal at least a range. It works as a legitimate filter, not a conversion blocker.
- **Price plus unexplained 'extra fees'** — A visible price followed by 'plus fees' or 'depends on customization' with no guidance makes the visitor distrust the number. If there are variables, show a range or explain what drives it (e.g., 'varies by number of users').
- **Price hidden behind a form** — A price tucked away on a secondary page or revealed only after submitting a form makes the visitor feel tricked. Transparency belongs on the main page or behind a clear link ('see pricing', 'view plans').
- **SaaS plans with 'contact us' instead of price** — Plans with no price, or with 'contact us' next to the CTA, block self-serve. If there's a plan, it should have a price; if there isn't, make it clear the offer is consultative.
