# Lowered risk for the visitor

> **Category:** Trust · **medium impact**
> Visitors are afraid of wasting time or money. How to offer a guarantee, a trial, or an answer to their biggest objection to convert.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/confianca/reducao-de-risco.md)

The biggest brake on conversion isn't 'how does it work?' — it's 'what if it doesn't work for me?'. When someone lands on a page for an unfamiliar product, perceived risk is the first barrier. Pages that offer a money-back guarantee, a no-credit-card trial, a free assessment, or a clear answer to the main objection convert significantly better. This article covers the ways to reduce risk and how to audit them.

## Why it matters

- In Don't Make Me Think, Steve Krug notes that visitors live in uncertainty and make fast decisions to minimize mental risk. For paid products, the financial objection ('am I going to lose money?') is almost always the first. Offering an anchor — 30-day money-back, a free trial, or an assessment that proves value before payment — signals confidence in the product and removes the worst objection.
- Cialdini's commitment principle shows that the smaller the initial ask, the greater the willingness to act. A 'try free for 7 days' works because the financial risk is zero; the objection shifts from 'I'll lose money' to 'I'll waste time' — a much smaller one. A page with no risk valve (no guarantee, no trial, no assessment) forces the visitor to bear 100% of the perceived risk.
- For services and SaaS, the most common form is a trial, with or without a card. For one-off purchases (ebooks, courses), it's a money-back guarantee. For consultative sales, it's a free assessment or call that proves value before the pitch. The format matters less than the existence and the clarity.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the page make clear what the visitor's main objection or risk is (money, time, or fit)?
- [ ] Is there a risk-reduction offer — guarantee, trial, free assessment, or '30 days back' — clearly signaled?
- [ ] Are the terms of the offer (timeframe, how to claim, no hidden fine print) legible above the fold or at the CTA?
- [ ] If it's a trial, does it specify 'no credit card required' or set the expectation of payment?
- [ ] If it's a guarantee, what's the window and how does the customer claim it (email, chat, an explicit process)?
- [ ] Is the offer placed NEAR the CTA or form, not isolated in a distant section?

## Common mistakes

- **Vague guarantee** — 'Satisfaction guaranteed' with no window or process raises doubt: 'guaranteed for how long? how do I claim it?'. Be explicit: '30-day money-back, no questions asked. Open a ticket here.'
- **Trial with no card clarity** — A 'free trial' that doesn't say whether a credit card is required leaves the risk in place (fear of being charged). If no card, highlight it. If a card is needed, say so upfront.
- **Risk reduction buried in the FAQ** — If the guarantee only lives in the footer or an FAQ page, visitors give up before they reach it. Risk reduction belongs above the fold or beside the primary CTA.
- **Offer that misses the real objection** — If the fear is 'I've never used a tool like this', offer onboarding or a demo. If it's price, offer a discounted annual plan. Match the offer to the actual objection.
