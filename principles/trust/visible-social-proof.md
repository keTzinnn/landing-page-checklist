# Visible social proof

> **Category:** Trust · **medium impact**
> Why 'great product' from an anonymous source falls flat. How to use specific, verifiable social proof to build credibility.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/confianca/prova-social-visivel.md)

Social proof is the psychological effect that makes people trust a product more when they see others already using and approving of it. On landing pages, that proof usually shows up — but often so vaguely that it hurts more than it helps. A testimonial with no name, company, or context, or a 'customer count' with no verifiable basis, gives the opposite impression: that the company is hiding something. This article covers why generic social proof drains trust and how to audit the quality of yours.

## Why it matters

- Cialdini (Influence) shows that social proof lowers perceived risk when it's concrete — when the visitor can identify and mentally validate the source. A named testimonial with a real role and company fires the 'a peer recommended this' mechanism. Abstract numbers ('10,000 users') don't trigger it; specific proof ('used by 10+ person agencies in São Paulo') does.
- The reverse error is rarely discussed: bad social proof corrodes trust. A generic quote, an unknown 'client' logo, or a testimonial that looks lifted from a template signals incompetence or dishonesty. Optimization tests with fake or vague testimonials often show a drop in conversion — no proof is better than dubious proof.
- For B2B and lead-capture pages, specific social proof works as a risk anchor: it answers the 'I've never heard of this company' objection with real precedents. The quality — name, context, result — determines whether it lowers or amplifies perceived risk.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does every identifiable testimonial have a full name, role, and/or a verifiable company?
- [ ] If there's a 'customer count' or volume figure, is there a clear source or criterion (e.g., 'since 2019', 'across 3 countries')?
- [ ] Are testimonials anchored to a specific result ('cut time from X to Y') or just generic ('great')?
- [ ] Are the 'client' logos real brands relevant to your target audience?
- [ ] Is there a way to validate the identity mentioned (profile link, website, press)?
- [ ] Do video or photo testimonials have a visible name and context (not disguised or boilerplate)?

## Common mistakes

- **Anonymous initials** — 'John S., São Paulo' lowers credibility. The visitor can't verify it and suspects it's fabricated. A full name plus context is the bare minimum.
- **'10,000 happy users' with no basis** — A floating number with no period, segment, or verification is just noise. Specify: 'customers since 2020', 'across creative agencies'.
- **Client logos with no context** — If the client means nothing to the viewer, the logo doesn't reduce risk. Group logos by segment or add a line of context.
- **Testimonials that read like a template** — 'Revolutionary', 'best on the market' — with no specifics, it sounds written by the company, not the customer. Look for real, genuine-sounding phrasing.
