# A clear, structured value proposition

> **Category:** Clarity · **maximum impact**
> A confusing value proposition loses the visitor. Clear structure (value + hook + persona) converts; disorganization distracts. How to audit and fix it.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/clareza/proposta-valor-estruturada.md)

A value proposition isn't a paragraph — it's a machine. It has three gears that all have to be visible: (1) How the customer gets value — the action that solves their problem. (2) A hook — a detail that grabs them, an objection answered, a reason to believe you're different. (3) The persona — exactly who this is for. When one of those fails, the proposition falls apart. The visitor reads it, half-gets it, and leaves. Missing structure isn't a flaw of style; it's a flaw of effectiveness.

## Why it matters

- The human brain processes stories in familiar shapes. A sale lands when it follows a clear plot: where you are now (problem) + the change (solution) + the result (benefit). An unstructured value proposition breaks that pattern — the visitor keeps hunting for the page's 'point' and leaves before they find it. Explicit structure (how it works + why it's different + who it's for) makes the cognitive processing more direct and more trustworthy.
- The hook — the objection or the difference — is what sets your solution apart from ten others promising the same thing. 'Win back 10 hours a week' is a generic promise; 'Win back 10 hours a week WITHOUT manually wiring it into your CRM' is specific and answers a real objection (integration is a pain). A good hook isn't makeup; it's the answer to a real doubt.
- Persona matters. A proposition written for 'tech companies' confuses both a 3-developer startup and a 200-engineer enterprise. When you name the persona ('For CTOs at B2B startups'), the right visitor identifies instantly; the wrong one leaves without friction — which is good, it saves your support team later.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the value proposition communicate HOW the customer gets value (e.g. 'automates reports,' 'cuts analysis time') in a single sentence, or does it stay abstract?
- [ ] Is there a hook — a reason to believe you're different: an objection answered, a unique angle, the thing that answers 'why not just use the solution that already exists'?
- [ ] Does the page name the persona/target audience it was built for, or does it talk about 'companies' and 'users' generically?
- [ ] Can the visitor follow the proposition in order — problem → solution → gain — or do they have to connect the dots themselves?
- [ ] If you read only the headline plus the first section, do you know exactly how the product solves the problem, or do you need to read more to be sure?

## Common mistakes

- **A proposition with no differentiating hook** — 'The platform that manages your projects' is generic; there are 100 of them online. Add the hook: '...without leaving Slack' or '...in under 2 minutes per project.'
- **Value described as features, not gains** — 'Integrates with 50+ tools' is a feature. The value is: 'All your data in one place, without switching tools.' The visitor doesn't want features; they want the result.
- **Missing or generic persona** — 'For companies that want to grow' describes every company on the planet. Be specific: 'For marketing agencies of 5-50 people who lose clients to late deliveries.'
- **Structure scattered across 5 sections** — The value proposition shouldn't sit in one block — it should be echoed and sharpened across the page. If the visitor can't reconstruct the key passage in a single read, the structure is weak.
