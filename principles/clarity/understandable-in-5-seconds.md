# A page you get in 5 seconds

> **Category:** Clarity · **maximum impact**
> Your visitor doesn't read. Within 5-10 seconds they've already decided whether to stay or go. How to check that your page passes the snap elevator-pitch test.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/clareza/entendivel-em-5s.md)

The visitor who landed on your page from an ad has 5 to 10 seconds of raw attention. If they can't answer 'What is this for?' in the blink of an eye, they leave. Not because the page is bad — because their brain is in scanning mode, not reading mode. Clarity in 5 seconds isn't elegance; it's survival. If you fail the ultra-fast legibility test, the rest of the page doesn't matter, because no one reads it.

## Why it matters

- Jakob Nielsen's classic studies show that most users scan — they read in 'search' mode rather than word by word (Nielsen Norman Group, 'How Little Do Users Read?'). The behavior is even more extreme on paid-traffic landing pages: the visitor paid to get there and wants to validate fast whether the click was worth it. If that validation fails in the first 5 seconds, the bounce is a reflex, not carelessness.
- The 5-second test isn't theoretical — it's cognitive. In scanning mode, the eye hunts for visual anchors (a big headline, contrasting colors) and keywords (benefit names, numbers). If the page is dense, disorganized, or full of competing anchors, the scan fails — the visitor can't extract the quick answer and bounces. The frustration is instant.
- Passing the 5-second test doesn't guarantee conversion, but failing it guarantees the loss. It's an entry filter; whoever fails here never reaches the form. The payoff of ultra-fast clarity on landing pages is well documented — it's the prerequisite for everything else.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Reading nothing but the headline and the main visual, can a stranger answer 'who is this for' and 'what does it do'?
- [ ] Is the headline visible on the first screen (above the fold) and sized and contrasted enough to jump out?
- [ ] Are there at most 2-3 visual elements above the fold, or is there so much information that attention has nowhere to land?
- [ ] If you muted the page and just looked (no reading), does the main visual communicate something about the value, or is it a generic stock photo?
- [ ] Can a cold visitor coming from an ad connect the ad's promise to the page headline without confusion, or is there a mismatch?

## Common mistakes

- **Generic or invisible headline** — An 18px headline in gray #999 on a white background doesn't jump out in 5 seconds. Bump the size and contrast or it gets skipped.
- **Too much above the fold** — 6+ sections, 3 CTAs, 20+ lines of copy and 5 rotating images create visual paralysis. The scan can't lock onto the purpose in 5 seconds.
- **Ad-to-page disconnect** — The ad promises 'Cut your effort by 70%'; the page says 'Manage projects better.' The visitor doesn't make the connection fast and bounces.
- **Visual that doesn't show value** — A stock photo of people smiling around a laptop says nothing about the product or benefit. Use a screenshot, a visual demo, or a diagram that shows the outcome.
