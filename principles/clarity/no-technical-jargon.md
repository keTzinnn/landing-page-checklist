# Plain language, no technical jargon

> **Category:** Clarity · **maximum impact**
> Technical jargon pushes visitors away. How to check whether your hero headline and copy speak the customer's language, not the engineer's.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/clareza/ausencia-de-jargao.md)

A page packed with technical jargon or empty corporate-speak is a door slammed in the visitor's face. When your site says 'Scalable cloud infrastructure with serverless architecture,' the visitor who isn't an engineer gives up before getting to the point. Worse: even if they stick around, they never grasp the real benefit. The rule is simple — speak the language your customer speaks, not your company's internal dialect. If the customer says 'I need more room to store my data,' don't answer with 'Distributed storage across multiple availability zones.'

## Why it matters

- Technical jargon has a job: fast communication between specialists. But on a landing page it works as a barrier. The visitor who doesn't know the jargon does one of two things: (1) assumes the product isn't for them and leaves, or (2) gets confused and leaves. Both are churn. Web-reading research (Nielsen, Krug) shows that visitors in scanning mode skip right over jargon — it's as if it weren't there. Plain text plus jargon equals a visitor who tunes the jargon out.
- Vague corporate-speak ('digital transformation,' 'innovative solutions,' 'resource optimization') is jargon and empty of meaning on top of it. Any company on earth could say the exact same line. Ogilvy called this 'flatulent puffery' — words that bloat the copy without adding value. They take up the space that should be communicating a benefit.
- The clarity test is simple: could your grandmother read the headline and understand what you sell? If the answer is no, there's jargon or corporate-speak hiding in there. This isn't an elitist standard — it's a standard of effectiveness. The simpler and more specific the language, the more people understand it and convert.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Do the headline and first block of copy use words a stranger to your industry would understand, or are there 3+ unexplained technical terms?
- [ ] If you swapped the technical terms for plainer alternatives, would the page get clearer, or would it lose essential precision?
- [ ] Are there empty adjectives ('innovative,' 'revolutionary,' 'world-class,' 'excellence') with no concrete benefit attached to back them up?
- [ ] Is the benefit stated in the customer's language (what problem it solves, how much time it saves, what relief they feel) or in the engineer's language (how the solution works under the hood)?
- [ ] If you asked a colleague from another field to read the headline, could they sum up the value in one natural sentence, or would they ask you to explain?

## Common mistakes

- **Untranslated technical jargon** — 'Scalable multi-tenant SaaS architecture with a REST API' is stacked jargon. Translate it: 'Grows with your business without making you switch tools.'
- **Vague, generic corporate-speak** — 'Transform your organization with next-generation solutions' could be said by any company alive. Get specific: 'Cuts new-client onboarding time by 60%.'
- **Confusing mix of jargon and customer language** — 'Our platform with advanced machine learning helps you sell faster' blends the tech (ML) with the benefit (sell faster). Pick one point of view.
- **Vague benefit description** — 'Optimizes your workflows' — which workflows? Optimized how? Be concrete: 'Automates client reminders, saving each manager 3 hours a week.'
