# A headline with a concrete benefit

> **Category:** Clarity · **maximum impact**
> A vague headline doesn't sell. How to build a headline that states a specific, testable benefit instead of generic corporate speak.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/clareza/headline-comunica-valor.md)

The headline is the first contract between your page and your visitor. When it promises something abstract ('Transform your business,' 'Innovative solutions'), the reader's brain has no idea what to expect — and it hesitates. When it's specific ('Cuts your data-analysis time by 70%'), the visitor recognizes the relevance instantly. The difference isn't tone — it's cognitive clarity. A generic headline hands the work of interpretation to someone who's already on the fence about staying.

## Why it matters

- Attention is the scarcest resource on the web. The savvy visitor skims — Jakob Nielsen's famous 5-second rule. If the headline doesn't communicate a concrete, differentiated benefit, the brain files the page under 'generic' and moves on. Vague corporate-speak ('World-class solutions,' 'Excellence in service') doesn't work as a relevance filter; it sounds like everything else.
- Specificity lowers cognitive friction. When you say 'Win back 3 hours a week on admin work,' the visitor runs the math: 'Do I need to win back time? Yes. How much is that worth to me? A lot.' The promise is testable and personal. Vague headlines ('We boost productivity') force the visitor to guess whether the promise applies to them — and guesses turn into objections.
- Eye-tracking research (Nielsen Norman Group) shows the headline is read first and decides whether the user continues or skips. A specific headline with a tangible benefit lifts time on page. The absence of specificity doesn't cost you conversion in any neatly measurable way — it costs you the initial trust that earns the right to be read further.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the headline state a SPECIFIC benefit (number, timeframe, outcome) your audience recognizes as relevant, or does it just lean on vague adjectives like 'better,' 'innovative,' 'advanced'?
- [ ] Can a visitor who's never heard of your product understand exactly what they'll gain from the headline alone, or do they have to read the copy below to make sense of it?
- [ ] Does the headline avoid empty corporate terms ('transformation,' 'solutions,' 'optimization') that aren't anchored to a concrete benefit?
- [ ] Is the benefit differentiated — not a promise any competitor could make word for word?
- [ ] If you swapped in a competitor's brand, would the headline still communicate value, or does it lean on brand recognition to make sense?

## Common mistakes

- **Generic abstractions instead of a promise** — 'Transform your business with smart solutions' explains neither HOW nor FOR WHAT. The visitor can't tell if it's for sales, support, finance, or ops.
- **Superlatives with no metric** — 'The best email software' or 'Revolutionary in productivity' give the visitor nothing to validate. Try: 'Delivers 2x faster than competitors' (testable).
- **Benefit buried in jargon** — 'Scalable cloud infrastructure' describes the mechanism, not the payoff. The customer doesn't want cloud; they want less downtime or to scale without babysitting a server.
- **A message aimed at 'everyone'** — 'The software everyone should use' speaks to no one in particular. A 5-person shop and a 5,000-person holding company have incompatible needs; the generic headline loses both.
