# Who the page is for, said plainly

> **Category:** Clarity · **maximum impact**
> Pages that speak to 'everyone' convert no one. How to name the persona so the right visitor sees themselves on the page.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/clareza/publico-alvo-explicito.md)

Every landing page is a conversation. When the copy speaks to generic 'companies' or 'users,' the visitor is left wondering: 'Is this even for me?' That uncertainty kills conversion. When you name the persona outright — 'For CTOs at fast-growing startups' or 'For freelance copywriters with B2B clients' — the right reader sees themselves immediately. The wrong reader leaves too, which is good: it saves your support team later. Specificity about who you're for is a filter that lifts conversion in the right segment and cuts out the noise.

## Why it matters

- Copywriting psychology tells us people process messages through personal relevance. When you say 'For marketing agencies,' a 5-person agency (that can't afford your $5k/month SaaS) isn't sure it's for them; a 200-person agency in a major city tunes out because it assumes you mean small shops. Specificity ('For agencies of 20-150 people with an established inbound program') removes that ambiguity. The right visitor feels the page was built for them; the wrong one leaves without frustration.
- When people see a message they feel is personally relevant, they engage with more attention and less skepticism. A page that names the persona specifically triggers that relevance processing. A generic page triggers defensive processing instead ('this is generic, it's not for me').
- For the business, audience specificity lowers acquisition costs because your ads and paid traffic are targeted, not sprayed. And it lowers churn, because you attract the people who actually fit your product instead of anyone who happens to click.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Does the page name the persona/segment explicitly ('For CTOs...', 'If you run an agency...', 'For freelancers who...'), or does it talk about 'companies' or 'users' generically?
- [ ] Does the named persona include a demographic trait (company size, role), a situational one (already uses tool X, or new to this?), or a psychographic one (the value they chase)?
- [ ] If you stripped out all the copy and left only the images/people shown, could you still tell who the product is for? Do the photos/avatars/customer examples reflect the persona?
- [ ] Is the objection or problem the page raises specific to the named persona, or generic enough to apply to several audiences?
- [ ] If you're in the wrong audience, can you leave quickly without frustration — or does the page make you waste time trying to figure out if you fit?

## Common mistakes

- **Vague or denied persona** — 'For companies of any size' or 'For anyone who wants to be productive' isn't specific; it's an abdication. Pick a segment — startups, SMBs, enterprises — and speak to it.
- **Persona named, but copy doesn't follow** — The headline says 'For marketing agencies,' but the copy talks about use cases in sales, HR, and IT. The inconsistency confuses; who's the audience again?
- **Competing personas on one page** — One section for startups, another for enterprises, another for freelancers. The visitor doesn't know which path to follow; the page turns to mush.
- **Generic images/avatars that don't match the persona** — A page for 'CTOs in tech' with photos of people in corporate suits creates visual dissonance; choose images that reflect the actual persona.
