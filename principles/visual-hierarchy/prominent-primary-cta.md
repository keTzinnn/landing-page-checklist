# A Primary Button That Stands Out

> **Category:** Visual Hierarchy · **medium-high impact**
> A visually dominant CTA converts better. Learn what makes a button stand out, why it matters, and how to check yours.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/hierarquia-visual/cta-primario-destacado.md)

The call to action is the bridge between interest and action. If it's visually subordinate to everything else — tiny, washed out, lost in a thicket of text — visitors may never realize there's anything to click. A standout primary button isn't decoration: it's the focal point that turns reading into movement. This principle measures whether your CTA was built to dominate, not to disappear.

## Why it matters

- Human visual perception favors contrast and scale. Nielsen Norman Group documents that attention follows larger elements and colors that are more saturated or opposite the background. When the primary button is the same size as body copy (typically 16px) or shares the background color, it competes on equal footing with subordinate content — forcing cognitive effort just to find it.
- Eye-tracking research confirms that fresh eyes scan headlines, images, and large elements first. A small, pale button is invisible in that initial sweep — the usability phenomenon known as a 'false floor.' The cost isn't only conversion: it's wasted ground. You earned the visit, but offered no visible way forward.
- Size (roughly 2x the body text) and contrast don't guarantee conversion, but their absence guarantees underuse. This is a design priority, not overkill — primary buttons in high-stakes applications (surgery, engineering) are made obvious for exactly this reason.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Is the primary button visibly larger than body text (about 1.5-2x)?
- [ ] Is there clear color contrast between the button and the background (not just a shift in shade)?
- [ ] Is the primary button the first action element that catches the eye above the fold?
- [ ] Are there no other buttons or CTAs above it competing for the same visual prominence?
- [ ] Is the button color distinct from the text and background palette, with no ambiguity?

## Common mistakes

- **Gray button on a white background** — Not enough visual contrast; the eye mistakes it for disabled text or a secondary link.
- **Button the same size as the running text** — The scale hierarchy doesn't come through; it gets lost in the paragraph.
- **Several same-size, same-color buttons above the fold** — Visitors can't tell which action is primary; the extra cognitive effort lowers click-through.
- **Button with a transparent fill or thin outline** — Without a solid fill and contrast, it's unclear whether it's even clickable.
