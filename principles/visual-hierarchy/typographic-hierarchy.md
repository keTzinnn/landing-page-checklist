# Typography That Organizes the Read

> **Category:** Visual Hierarchy · **medium-high impact**
> A flat, monotone font makes a page feel generic. Learn how type scale and weight create a readable hierarchy.
>
> 🇧🇷 [Versão em português](../../principios/hierarquia-visual/tipografia-hierarquica.md)

Typography isn't decoration — it's the infrastructure of comprehension. Headlines at a clear size (48-64px), subheads at a mid scale (24-32px), body at 16-18px: that visual progression builds a path the eye follows naturally. When the whole page sits at 16px regular with no variation, the hierarchy disappears and everything competes equally. Legibility improves, comprehension speeds up, and the product's tone comes through when typography is hierarchical — each level of content sized and weighted to match its importance.

## Why it matters

- The neuroscience of reading shows that sharp changes in size and weight reorient attention; the brain registers the difference and prioritizes the larger element. It's pre-conscious — visitors don't 'think,' they just flow. Headlines at an appropriate scale read as more relevant than body copy, and the shift in weight (regular vs. bold) reinforces the hierarchy.
- Type weight (regular, bold, heavy) conveys importance. A bold headline says 'read this'; regular body copy keeps reading fluid. Deliberate variation — not randomness — creates a pattern that aids scanning, so visitors learn where to look for the critical information.
- Beyond comprehension, typography sets the tone. A classic serif reads premium and serious. A geometric sans-serif reads modern and tech. Script or hand-drawn reads as personality and trust. 'Flat' typography (every font identical, generic sans-serif, no contrast) makes a page feel like a template, not a brand. Done well, typographic hierarchy reinforces brand and credibility.

## How to check your page

- [ ] Are headlines visibly larger than subheads (at least a 1.5x scale)?
- [ ] Are subheads larger than body text (at least a 1.3x scale)?
- [ ] Is body text legible on mobile (at least 16px, no smaller)?
- [ ] Is there weight variation between levels (bold headline, regular body) or only size?
- [ ] Does the typography match the product's tone (tech = sans-serif; premium = serif; creative = display)?

## Common mistakes

- **Whole page in 16px regular sans-serif with no distinct sizes** — Zero visual hierarchy; the entire page is noise; it looks like a generic template.
- **Headline set small (20px) while the logo is 40px** — Inverts the hierarchy; the logo isn't the promise; it confuses what matters.
- **Multiple typefaces (3+ different fonts) with no clear system** — Visual chaos; no rule is legible; the brand reads as incoherent.
- **Body lines of 80+ characters at 14px with weak contrast** — Eye strain while reading; visitors abandon the page before they get it.
